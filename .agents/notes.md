# Notes

Design decisions, research findings, and rationale.

## 2026-04-03 — Porcelanic countertops

Porcelanic slabs (also called sintered stone or large-format porcelain) are a strong candidate for kitchen countertops:

- **Heat resistant**: Can place hot pots directly on the surface without damage (withstands >1000C in manufacturing)
- **Chemical resistant**: Non-porous, resistant to acids, stains, and cleaning agents
- **Scratch resistant**: Very hard surface (Mohs ~6-7)
- **Cheap**: Significantly cheaper than natural stone (granite, marble) or engineered quartz. Typical range 40-80 EUR/m2 for material, vs 100-300+ for quartz/granite
- **Thin profiles available**: 6mm, 12mm, 20mm — thinner = lighter and cheaper
- **Large format**: Slabs up to 3.2m x 1.6m, meaning fewer seams
- **UV resistant**: Won't yellow or fade outdoors (relevant if used near windows or outdoor kitchen)

**Brands to research**: Neolith, Dekton (Cosentino), Laminam, Sapienstone
**Considerations**: Edge finishing can be tricky with thin slabs; need reinforcement for undermount sinks; some installers may be less familiar with it vs quartz

## 2026-05-23 — Drawers vs. boxes on shelving

Refines the existing "use shelvings rather than drawers" rule (in `docs/layout/bathroom.md`).

- **Small drawers should not exist.** A small drawer's job is done better by a box sitting on a shelf.
- A box on a shelf beats a small drawer: you can pull it out and **carry it to wherever you're working**, and **put a lid on it** to keep contents clean/contained. No drawer hardware needed.
- Nice-to-have on the box: a bit of **resistance/damping when pulling it out** so it doesn't slam out of the shelf.
- **Exception — frequent, one-handed access**: real drawers win here (soft-close, single-motion open without lifting a box out). Use drawers only where access is frequent and one-handedness matters; default to boxes-on-shelving everywhere else.
